Executive AI Briefing — Presentation Script

A word-for-word guide for delivering the 90-minute executive AI briefing. Use this alongside the slides and presenter guide.

Pre-Session Checklist

Before you begin: - [ ] Test slides in presentation mode - [ ] Have notepad ready for parking lot items - [ ] Calculator app open for the interactive exercise - [ ] Water available - [ ] Research their company completed

OPENING (10 minutes)

Slide 1: Title (30 seconds)

[Click to Slide 1]

“Thank you for making time for this today.”

“Over the next 90 minutes, I want to give you something you can actually use — a mental framework for thinking about AI that cuts through the noise.”

“This isn’t going to be a technology lecture. It’s a conversation about your business and where AI fits.”

[Transition quickly — this is just the opener]

Slide 2: Monday Morning, Two Companies (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 2]

“Let me paint a picture for you.”

“Imagine two companies. Same size. Same industry. Same Monday morning. But very different days.”

[Walk through the table row by row. Don’t rush — let each contrast land.]

“Customer emails — Company A has 200+ emails to sort through manually every morning. Company B? Auto-categorized, prioritized, with draft responses ready to review.”

“Sales forecasting — Company A is updating spreadsheets weekly, and they’re often wrong. Company B has a forecast that updates itself daily in real-time.”

“Quote turnaround — Company A makes customers wait 48 hours. Company B generates quotes in 4 hours.”

“Document processing — Company A spends 15 hours a week on manual data entry. That’s almost half a person just on data entry. Company B? Extracted automatically, 3 hours to review.”

“Customer questions — Company A’s team is overwhelmed with slow response times. Company B handles 70% of those questions instantly with AI.”

[Pause]

“The difference between these two companies isn’t budget. It isn’t headcount. It’s knowing which AI to use where.”

“That’s what we’re going to cover today.”

[Watch for which rows they react to most — that signals their pain points]

Slide 3: Three Shifts That Changed Everything (4 minutes)

[Click to Slide 3]

“So why now? Why is AI suddenly accessible to companies of all sizes?”

“Three things changed.”

[Walk through each shift]

“First, cost. Five years ago, this was Fortune 500 only. The technology was expensive, the talent was scarce, and the infrastructure was prohibitive. Now? A small business can access the same technology for a fraction of the cost.”

“Second, accessibility. You don’t need a PhD or a data science team anymore. There are plug-and-play tools, APIs, no-code platforms. The barrier to entry has collapsed.”

“Third — and this is the one that matters most — expectation. Your customers expect it. Your employees expect it. AI is becoming table stakes, not a competitive advantage.”

[Pause for emphasis]

“This isn’t about being first. It’s about not being last.”

[If they ask about competitors, park the question: “Great question — let me capture that and we’ll come back to it.”]

Slide 4: The AI Iceberg (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 4]

“This image captures what we’re going to explore today.”

[Point to the visual]

“Above the waterline is what everyone talks about — ChatGPT, image generators, Copilot. The trending stuff. The headlines.”

“Below the waterline is what’s been quietly driving business value for years. Machine learning. Computer vision. Speech recognition. Robotics and automation. Optimization systems.”

“Here’s what most people miss: that stuff below the waterline? It’s been in production for 20+ years. It’s proven. It’s boring. And it’s often where the fastest ROI lives.”

[Checkpoint question]

“Does this framing match your experience? Or is this surprising?”

[Listen to their response. This tells you whether they’ve bought into the core premise.]

BLOCK 1: The AI Landscape (18 minutes)

Slide 5: Section Divider (15 seconds)

[Click to Slide 5]

“Let’s start by understanding what we’re actually talking about when we say ‘AI.’”

Slide 6: AI = Machines That Learn From Data (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 6]

“The simplest way to think about AI versus traditional software:”

“Traditional software follows rules that humans write. IF this, THEN that. Every edge case, every decision — a human had to anticipate it and code it.”

“AI is different. It learns from examples. You show it patterns, and it figures out how to apply those patterns to new situations.”

[Point to the two cards]

“Traditional: IF this THEN that. Humans write every rule.”

“AI: Learn, find patterns, apply. The machine learns from examples.”

“The key insight here — and this is important — different types of AI learn different things from different types of data. That’s why there isn’t one AI. There’s a family of them.”

Slide 7: Four Types of AI (10 minutes)

[Click to Slide 7]

“Let me introduce you to the AI family. Four types that drive business value.”

[Walk through each card — this is a core teaching slide, take your time]

Predictive AI: > “First, Predictive AI — AI that sees the future. This is the oldest and most mature type. It forecasts outcomes, scores probability, and optimizes decisions.” > > “You’ve been using this for years without knowing it. Netflix recommendations? Predictive AI. Credit card fraud alerts? Predictive AI. Airline ticket pricing that changes every time you look? Predictive AI.” > > “For business, think demand forecasting, churn prediction, lead scoring, predictive maintenance. This has been in production for 20+ years. Very mature, very proven.”

Perception AI: > “Second, Perception AI — AI that sees, hears, and reads. It interprets images, transcribes speech, and understands text.” > > “Face unlock on your phone? Perception AI seeing your face. Zoom transcription? Perception AI hearing the call. Your spam filter? Perception AI reading emails and deciding what’s junk.” > > “For business: quality inspection, document processing, call center analytics, contract review. Also very mature — 10+ years in production.”

Generative AI: > “Third, Generative AI — AI that creates. This is the new kid on the block. The one making headlines.” > > “ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, DALL-E, GitHub Copilot. It writes text, creates images, generates code.” > > “For business: content creation, first drafts, code assistance, brainstorming. Medium maturity — 2-3 years in its current form, but improving rapidly.”

Physical AI: > “Fourth, Physical AI — AI that moves and acts in the real world. This combines robotics, autonomous systems, and process automation.” > > “Warehouse robots at Amazon. Self-driving features in your car. Automated workflows that move data between systems without human intervention.” > > “For business: manufacturing, logistics, process automation. Medium maturity — requires significant investment, but transformative when it fits.”

[Checkpoint question]

“Quick gut check: which of these four feels most relevant to your business right now?”

[Write down their answer — this will guide the rest of the session]

Slide 8: How They Work Together (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 8]

“Here’s what makes this interesting: the most powerful solutions combine multiple AI types. Let me show you two examples.”

[Point to the first example]

“Modern customer service: When a customer calls, Perception AI transcribes the call and understands what they’re asking for. Predictive AI identifies the best resolution and routes them appropriately. Generative AI drafts the response or surfaces the right knowledge article.”

“Three types of AI, working together, in a single workflow.”

[Point to the second example]

“Intelligent document workflow: Perception AI reads and extracts data from incoming documents — invoices, contracts, applications. Predictive AI categorizes them, flags exceptions, and routes them to the right person. Generative AI summarizes the key points and can answer questions about the content.”

“When you hear ‘AI solution,’ it’s usually not one thing — it’s a combination. That’s also where off-the-shelf tools start to fall short and custom solutions start to make more sense.”

BLOCK 2: AI Capabilities (17 minutes)

Slide 9: Section Divider (15 seconds)

[Click to Slide 9]

“Now that you know the AI types, let’s make it practical. How do you match AI to your actual problems?”

Slide 10: Capability Framework (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 10]

“AI can do six things for business: See, Hear, Read, Predict, Create, and Act. Each maps to a type of AI.”

[Point to each capability quickly — this is a reference framework]

“See — interpret visual information. That’s Perception AI.”

“Hear — process audio and speech. Perception AI.”

“Read — understand text and documents. Perception AI.”

“Predict — forecast future outcomes. Predictive AI.”

“Create — generate new content. Generative AI.”

“Act — take physical or digital action. Physical AI.”

“The reason I’m showing you this framework is because it changes how you think about problems. Instead of asking ‘What AI should I use?’ you ask ‘What capability do I need?’”

Slide 11: Matching Problems to AI (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 11]

“Here’s how to use that framework. Start with your problem, then find the capability you need.”

[Walk through 2-3 examples]

“If your problem is ‘We can’t process invoices fast enough’ — you need to SEE the invoice and READ the data. That’s Document AI, which is Perception AI.”

“If your problem is ‘We don’t know which customers will churn’ — you need to PREDICT. That’s Predictive ML.”

“If your problem is ‘Our call center is overwhelmed’ — you need to HEAR customer questions and CREATE responses. That’s Conversational AI — a combination of Perception and Generative.”

“Any of these sound like problems you’re dealing with?”

[Listen — this primes them for the interactive exercise later]

Slide 12: What AI Cannot Do (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 12]

“Now let me be honest about what AI can’t do. This is important because the vendors won’t tell you this.”

[Walk through the limitations table]

“Predictive AI — it’s only as good as historical data. It can’t predict black swan events or paradigm shifts. If your business is changing fundamentally, your historical data won’t help.”

“Perception AI — it struggles with edge cases. The weird invoice format, the accent it hasn’t heard before, the handwriting that’s barely legible. It needs human review for exceptions.”

“Generative AI — hallucinations. It can be confidently, convincingly wrong. You must verify facts and outputs. Never publish or act on generative AI output without review.”

“Physical AI — expensive and inflexible. Best for controlled, repetitive environments. Not great at adapting to chaos.”

[Point to the Universal Truths]

“And some things are true for all AI:”

“Garbage in, garbage out. Data quality determines AI quality.”

“AI has no judgment. It can’t weigh ethical tradeoffs. That’s still your job.”

“AI doesn’t actually understand. It’s sophisticated pattern matching, not comprehension.”

“Security matters. AI systems need governance and oversight.”

“Knowing limitations makes you a smarter buyer and user. The best implementations build in guardrails from day one.”

[Checkpoint question]

“Before we move on — any of these capabilities feel unclear? Anything you want me to explain differently?”

BREAK (5 minutes)

[Click to Break slide if you have one, or stay on slide 12]

“Let’s take a quick 5-minute break. When we come back, I want to talk specifically about your business.”

“Grab some coffee, check your phone — we’ll resume in five.”

[During the break:] - Review your notes on what they’ve said so far - Prepare to connect their situation to the framework - Have the calculator ready for the interactive exercise

BLOCK 3: Business Impact (18 minutes)

Slide 13: Section Divider (15 seconds)

[Click to Slide 13]

[After they return from break]

“Before we dive in — anything from the first half you want me to clarify?”

[Address any questions, then continue]

“Good. Let’s connect this to real business outcomes.”

Slide 14: Let’s Make This About You — THE ANCHOR (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 14]

This is your most important slide. Be fully present.

“Before we look at frameworks and case studies, I want to make sure this is relevant to you — not just theory.”

[Ask the question. Then stop talking.]

“What’s the one process in your business that eats the most time for the least value? The thing your best people complain about. The task everyone dreads.”

[Wait for their answer. Give them space. This might take 30-60 seconds. Write down what they say.]

[If they struggle, prompt gently:] > “Think about… where does your team spend time on repetitive data entry?” > “What information do people ask for that takes too long to find?” > “Where do things slow down waiting for approvals or reviews?” > “What do you wish just updated itself automatically?”

[Once they identify a problem, do the math together:]

“Okay, let’s put a number on this. How many hours a week would you say people spend on this task?”

[Write down the number]

“And how many people are involved?”

[Write it down]

“What’s the fully loaded hourly cost for those roles? For most professional roles, figure $50 to $75 per hour including benefits and overhead.”

[Write it down]

“And that happens 52 weeks a year.”

[Do the multiplication on your calculator, or let them do it]

“So this one problem is costing you roughly $[X] per year. And that’s before we count the opportunity cost — the deals not closed, the customers not served, the strategy not developed because your people were doing [the task they mentioned].”

[Let that number sink in]

“Keep that number in mind. As we go through the next section, I want you to think about what cutting that number in half would mean for your business.”

Slide 15: Five Ways AI Creates Value (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 15]

“AI creates value in five ways: Cost reduction, revenue growth, risk mitigation, speed, and experience.”

[Quick tour of the five value types]

“Cost reduction — do more with less. Automation, optimization, predictive maintenance.”

“Revenue growth — sell more, sell better. Personalization, dynamic pricing, lead scoring.”

“Risk mitigation — prevent bad outcomes. Fraud detection, quality control, compliance.”

“Speed — faster decisions, faster actions. Real-time analytics, automated workflows.”

“Experience — delight customers and employees. 24/7 support, personalization, reduced friction.”

[Reference their problem]

“Based on what you told me about [their problem], we’re probably looking at [cost/speed/whatever applies] as the primary value driver.”

Slide 16: Case Study — Stitch Fix (90 seconds)

[Click to Slide 16]

“Let me show you three quick examples of companies doing this well.”

“First, Stitch Fix — they’re an online personal styling service, publicly traded company.”

“Their problem: how do you scale personalized fashion recommendations to millions of customers? You can’t hire that many human stylists.”

“Their solution: Predictive AI analyzes style preferences, purchase history, and fit data. Perception AI processes clothing images to match aesthetics.”

“The result: 80% of their revenue is driven by algorithmic recommendations. Their human stylists are 30% more efficient because AI does the heavy lifting.”

“This is Predictive plus Perception AI working together.”

Slide 17: Case Study — Klarna (90 seconds)

[Click to Slide 17]

“Second, Klarna — global payments provider, $6.7 billion valuation.”

“Their problem: 2.3 million customer service chats per month. Human agents couldn’t keep up.”

“Their solution: an AI assistant that handles full customer conversations, integrated with their systems.”

“The result: AI now handles two-thirds of all customer chats. That’s equivalent to the work of 700 human agents. And resolution time dropped from 11 minutes to 2 minutes.”

“This is Conversational AI — Perception to understand the question, Generative to craft the response.”

Slide 18: Case Study — JPMorgan COIN (90 seconds)

[Click to Slide 18]

“Third, JPMorgan COIN — the largest bank in the United States.”

“Their problem: lawyers were spending 360,000 hours per year reviewing commercial loan agreements. That’s over 170 full-time lawyers just reading contracts.”

“Their solution: the COIN platform — Contract Intelligence — reads and extracts data from legal documents automatically.”

“The result: work that took 360,000 human hours now takes seconds. And it makes fewer errors.”

“This is Perception AI — AI that reads, extracts, and categorizes.”

[Synthesis]

“Each of these stories matched the right type of AI to a specific problem. These aren’t startups — they’re established companies with verifiable results.”

[Checkpoint question]

“Any of those examples spark ideas about your own situation?”

Slide 19: AI + Humans (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 19]

“I want to address something that might be on your mind. The question isn’t AI versus humans. It’s how we combine them.”

[Walk through the two columns]

“AI does: process at scale, find patterns in data, execute consistently, work 24/7, generate options.”

“Humans do: set strategy and priorities, interpret meaning and context, handle exceptions and edge cases, build relationships, make final decisions.”

“The best implementations don’t replace humans. They augment human capability. Radiologists plus AI equals more accurate diagnoses. Lawyers plus AI equals more cases handled, more time for strategy. Marketers plus AI equals more personalization, more creativity.”

“The question isn’t ‘AI or humans?’ It’s ‘How do we combine them effectively?’”

BLOCK 4: Getting Started (17 minutes)

Slide 20: Section Divider (15 seconds)

[Click to Slide 20]

“Let’s get practical. What does it actually take to get started?”

Slide 21: Investment Framework (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 21]

“Most people think AI requires a massive investment. It doesn’t.”

[Walk through the ladder]

“Learn — free. Understand what’s possible for your business. That’s what we’re doing right now.”

“Experiment — zero to $500 a month. Try existing tools, validate the concept. ChatGPT, built-in AI features, free trials.”

“First Win — under $1,000. Get a ready-to-deploy solution for a common need. A FAQ chatbot, basic automation.”

“Custom Build — $2,000 to $10,000. A solution built for your specific workflow. Custom chatbot, document Q&A, automated reports.”

“Scale — $10,000 plus. Multiple workflows, integrations, AI agents working together.”

“You can get a working solution that solves a real problem for less than you’d spend on a single hire.”

Slide 22: The FIT Assessment (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 22]

“Here’s how I’d evaluate any AI opportunity: FIT.”

[Walk through each letter]

“F — Feasibility. Do we have the data? Is the technology mature enough for this use case?”

“I — Impact. Will this meaningfully move a business metric? Not just ‘cool’ — actually valuable.”

“T — Timeframe. Can we see results in a reasonable period? Quick wins versus strategic bets.”

[Reference their problem]

“For what you described — [their problem] — it sounds like Feasibility is [yes/maybe], Impact is [high/medium], and Timeframe could be [quick win/strategic bet].”

[Point to the two categories]

“Quick wins are proven AI, clear data, measurable impact, less than six months to results.”

“Strategic bets are newer AI, transformative potential, longer horizon.”

“Most companies should start with quick wins to build confidence and capability, then tackle strategic bets.”

Slide 23: Start Monday (5 minutes)

[Click to Slide 23]

“Based on what we discussed, here’s what I’d do if I were you.”

[Personalize this based on their situation. Reference the problem they identified.]

“This week: Try a free AI tool on that specific task we talked about — [reference their problem]. I’d recommend [specific tool based on their need]. Note what works and what doesn’t. And estimate: how much is this problem actually costing you? We did a quick calculation, but dig deeper.”

“This month: Audit your data for that use case. What do you have? What’s missing? Talk to the team members who do this task daily — they’ll tell you the real pain points. And consider: is this a ‘use existing tools’ problem or a ‘custom solution’ problem?”

“When you’re ready to go further: The jump from experimenting to implementation is where most companies get stuck. The key is having clarity on three things: What problem you’re solving. What data you have. What success looks like.”

CLOSING (5 minutes)

Slide 24: Three Takeaways (3 minutes)

[Click to Slide 24]

“If you remember nothing else from today…”

[Deliver each takeaway slowly]

“One: AI is a family, not a single tool. Predictive, Perception, Generative, Physical — each solves different problems. When someone says ‘AI,’ ask ‘Which type?’”

“Two: Most valuable AI is boring and proven. The hype is new; the value is established. Twenty years of machine learning. Ten years of computer vision. Start with proven AI for quick wins.”

“Three: Start with the problem, not the technology. ‘What hurts?’ before ‘What’s cool?’ Match the AI type to your specific challenge.”

[Pause. Then deliver the provocative question slowly.]

“And one question to take with you…”

“What would your team do with 10 extra hours per week?”

[Let it sit. Don’t answer it for them. Let them think.]

“That’s what AI can unlock. The question is what you’ll do with it.”

Slide 25: Where Do You Go From Here (2 minutes)

[Click to Slide 25]

“Three paths forward from here:”

“One — Explore on your own. You now have the framework. Try the tools, see what clicks. The resources page has curated tools by AI type and use case. No signup required.”

“Two — Go deeper on a topic. If something we discussed today sparked your interest, I’m happy to point you to more detailed resources, case studies, or people doing interesting work in that area.”

“Three — Talk through a specific challenge. If you want to think through that opportunity we identified — the [reference their problem] — a follow-up conversation can help clarify whether it’s worth pursuing and what the path might look like. Regardless of who implements it.”

[Closing thought — deliver with sincerity]

“The goal today wasn’t to sell you on AI. It was to give you a framework for thinking clearly about it.”

“The companies winning with AI aren’t chasing the latest trend — they’re matching the right AI to the right problem. Now you have the mental model to do that too.”

[End warmly]

“Thank you for your time. I’m happy to answer any final questions, or we can follow up later if something comes to mind.”

Handling Common Objections

They Say You Respond
“We’re too small for AI” “Small companies actually move faster. There’s less bureaucracy, faster decisions. And you can get a working solution for under $1K.”
“Our data is a mess” “Everyone’s is. You don’t need perfect data to start. Let’s identify what you do have and start there.”
“I don’t trust AI” “Healthy skepticism is good. That’s why we talked about limitations and guardrails. The key is human oversight — AI assists, humans decide.”
“My team won’t adopt it” “Change management is real. The trick is starting with the pain point they complain about most. If AI solves their biggest headache, adoption follows.”
“Isn’t this just hype?” “The generative stuff is new and evolving. But predictive and perception AI have been delivering ROI for 20+ years. The hype is new; the value is established.”
